Predictive Models (LSTM, GRU, DreamerV3)

üß† RNN & LSTM

  • RNNs process sequential data
  • LSTM uses gating to avoid vanishing gradients
  • May be heavy for very long sequences

üéõ GRU & Other Architectures

  • GRU: Simpler gating, often faster
  • Others: RAU, Transformers for advanced sequence modeling
  • Selection depends on data & computational limits

üåê DreamerV3: Model-Based RL

  • Learns a latent world model to plan in ‚Äúimagination‚Äù
  • Similar to VAE/MDN-RNN approaches
  • Ideal for partial-observation tasks

üîë Why Predictive Models Matter

  • Forecasts human motion & environmental changes
  • Key for safe and socially acceptable navigation
  • Enables advanced planning when combined with RL

Cosine-Gated LSTM (CGLSTM)

üìå Introduction

  • ‚úÖ Proposes a novel LSTM-based architecture with a cosine similarity gate.
  • ü§ñ Aims to improve sequence prediction by focusing on directional alignment.
  • üìå Builds on limitations observed in previous LSTM experiments (e.g., in SocNavGym).

üîç Fundamentals & Limitations

  • ‚úÖ LSTM overcomes vanishing gradients but struggles with long-term dependencies.
  • ü§ñ Sensitive to outliers and may not gate optimally in dynamic environments.
  • üìå Motivates enhancing the gating mechanism with cosine similarity.

üí° Why Cosine-Gated LSTM?

  • ‚úÖ Cosine similarity emphasizes vector orientation over magnitude.
  • ü§ñ Helps the model focus on the most relevant sequence features.
  • üìå Leads to more robust gating and improved long-term dependency modeling.

üèóÔ∏è CGLSTM Architecture

üåÄ Cosine Similarity Gate

  • ‚úÖ Gate-ic: Compares transformed input vs. previous output alignment.
  • ü§ñ Gate-co: Measures alignment with the current output state.
  • üìå Combined gating filters out noise and emphasizes relevant features.

üß™ Datasets & Evaluation

  • ‚úÖ FallingBallEnv: Tests fundamental motion prediction (balanced & unbalanced).
  • ü§ñ Benchmark tasks: Adding Problem, row-wise MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, IMDB, PTB.
  • üìå SocNavGym: Real-world-like social navigation scenario.
  • ‚úÖ Metrics: MAE/MSE, accuracy, perplexity, training/testing time, t-tests.

Adaptive Predictive Reinforcement Learning: Entropy‚ÄêDriven Adaptive Prediction Horizons

üìå Introduction

  • ‚úÖ Dynamically adjusts prediction horizons in continuous action spaces.
  • ü§ñ Uses policy entropy to gauge uncertainty and set planning depth.
  • üìå Extends our prior work on predictive world models for social navigation.

üí° Motivation

  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Fixed horizons waste computation in simple settings and underperform in complex ones.
  • üîç Real-world tasks vary in uncertainty‚Äîrequiring longer horizons when needed.
  • üéØ Entropy provides a quantitative measure of action uncertainty for dynamic planning.

Research Evolution

üöÄ From fixed horizons in discrete spaces to adaptive horizons in continuous domains.

üéØ Research Objectives & Contributions

  • ‚úÖ Propose an entropy-driven adaptive horizon framework within SAC.
  • ü§ñ Integrate CGLSTM into DreamerV3 for improved temporal modeling.
  • üìä Benchmark our approach against SAC, PPO, TD3, DDPG, and DreamerV3 variants.

Proposed Methods

üöÄ Proposed: DreamerV3 + CGLSTM

‚úÖ Replaces GRU cells with CGLSTM to enhance world model predictions and long-term state accuracy.

üîó Proposed: 2StepAhead-MASPM (Fixed Horizon)

ü§ñ Combines two-step ahead prediction with MASPM‚Äôs action evaluation using a fixed 8-step horizon.

ü§ñ Proposed: SAC + CGLSTM (Adaptive Horizon)

üìå Dynamically adjusts the prediction horizon based on policy entropy‚Äîplanning further when uncertain.

üß™ Preliminary Experiments Overview

  • üîç Evaluated in LunarLander‚Äëv2 to optimize window-slide and horizon settings.
  • ‚öôÔ∏è Tested window-slide sizes: 16, 32, 64 to balance accuracy and cost.
  • üéØ Compared adaptive horizon sets {1,2,3,4,5} vs. {1,2,4,6,8} against fixed 8-step horizon.

üìä Window-Slide Selection for CGLSTM

  • ‚úÖ Window-slide of 64 achieved lowest losses but was too costly.
  • ü§ñ Window-slide of 16 was insufficient for complex dynamics.
  • üéØ Selected a window-slide of 32 as the optimal trade-off.

üéØ Adaptive Horizon Selection

  • üîç Compared horizon sets: {1,2,3,4,5} vs. {1,2,4,6,8}.
  • ü§ñ Set {1,2,4,6,8} achieved higher final rewards (~280) versus ~240‚Äì250.
  • ‚ö° Adaptive horizons reached 500k steps ~6 hours faster than fixed 8-step.
